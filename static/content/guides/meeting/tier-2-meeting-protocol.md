# Meeting and Deciding at Tier 2: A Protocol for Integrative Coordination

*A practical guide to Yellow/Turquoise decision-making — and how to include everyone on the spiral.*

*This protocol is not a prescription but an invitation — a set of patterns to be adapted, abandoned, or reinvented as the situation demands. If it becomes a new orthodoxy, it has failed.*

---

## The fundamental shift

Tier 1 stages each embed a fixed relationship to how decisions get made. Blue follows procedure. Orange optimizes for the winning argument. Green seeks consensus and emotional inclusion. Each believes *its* mode is correct and experiences the others as dysfunction.

At Tier 2, this changes along several axes simultaneously:

- **Positions → Processes** — the focus shifts from defending views to designing how views interact
- **Opinions → Models** — participants offer testable framings of reality, not just preferences
- **Control → Coherence** — the aim is not to direct the outcome but to let the most functional outcome become visible
- **Debate → Exploration of reality** — disagreement becomes information, not threat

The core meta-move of Yellow/Turquoise coordination is this: **the group decides how to decide, and that itself becomes a conscious, flexible act rather than an inherited habit.**

Turquoise adds a further layer — collective sensing. Not just "what's the smartest systemic choice?" but "what's trying to emerge here that none of us individually see yet?"

If there is a single invariant principle beneath all of this, it is: **the purpose of the protocol is to increase the system's ability to perceive reality accurately and respond coherently.** Everything else — phases, roles, practices — is optional, adaptable, and context-sensitive. This principle is the anchor that prevents the protocol from becoming a new form of rigidity.

## When to use this protocol — and when not to

Not every decision warrants this level of process. Over-processing simple decisions will undermine credibility faster than anything else.

A quick filter: **Is the decision reversible?** If yes, bias toward action — try it, observe, adjust. Use authority or a quick advice process. Reserve the full protocol for decisions that are complex, high-stakes, or irreversible, where multiple value systems are genuinely in tension and the cost of getting it wrong is significant.

Similarly, this protocol assumes at least some psychological safety and shared purpose. In contexts dominated by acute survival stress (Beige/Red), begin with simpler structures that build trust, and gradually introduce elements as the ground stabilizes.

## Why Tier 1 meetings get stuck

Each Tier 1 stage has characteristic failure modes in group coordination:

- **Blue** defaults to procedure even when the situation demands adaptivity, and treats deviation as disorder
- **Orange** collapses complex decisions into optimization problems, often ignoring second- and third-order effects
- **Green** conflates *being heard* with *having influenced the outcome*, and can produce meeting paralysis when consensus proves impossible
- **Red** treats every meeting as a power contest, which can be energizing but tends to suppress the quieter signals

More fundamentally, Tier 1 stages *systematically avoid certain questions* — each stage protects its own blind spot. Green won't ask "is inclusion actually the priority here?" Orange won't ask "are we optimizing the wrong thing?" Blue won't ask "is the procedure itself the problem?"

Tier 2 meetings are defined by their willingness to surface exactly these questions.

## What Tier 2 meetings feel like

A Yellow/Turquoise meeting often feels:

- Slower at first, faster over time
- Less performative, more reflective
- Less adversarial, more co-creative
- More comfortable with uncertainty and unresolved tension

Two key experiential signatures:

**Asking difficult questions** — not to challenge *people*, but to challenge assumptions, system boundaries, hidden incentives, and second-order effects. Examples:

- "What problem are we *actually* trying to solve?"
- "What happens if we succeed?"
- "Who is not in the room that this decision affects?"
- "Which part of our identity are we protecting by not looking at this?"

**Allowing time for thinking** — silence becomes a feature, not a bug. Tier 2 recognizes that fast opinions ≠ good thinking. Complex, systemic integration rarely happens spontaneously in a noisy room. Reflection pauses, writing before speaking, and iterative rounds replace reactive debate.

## The protocol

This is not a rigid script — that would contradict the adaptive nature of Tier 2. It is a *pattern language* for meetings that can be entered at any phase and adapted to context.

### Phase 0: Systemic preparation (before the meeting)

The most important work of a Tier 2 meeting often happens before anyone gathers. This is where the facilitator metabolizes complexity so the group doesn't have to do it live:

- **Stakeholder mapping** — who is affected? Who holds relevant perspectives? Who is likely to feel threatened?
- **Value system scanning** — what vMemes are likely to be present or activated? Where are potential collisions?
- **Decision mode pre-framing** — what mode *might* fit? (Held lightly, to be confirmed or adjusted by the group)
- **Complexity read** — is this simple, complicated, or complex? Does it involve a solvable problem or an ongoing polarity?

A well-prepared facilitator makes the meeting feel almost effortless — because the complexity was metabolized beforehand. This preparation is private and provisional, not imposed.

### Phase 1: Ground the field

Brief orientation. Not the Green "share your feelings" circle, but not pure Orange agenda-setting either. The question is:

> *Where is your attention right now? What are you bringing into this room?*

This serves multiple functions: it gives the facilitator a read on the group's state, it creates a moment of arrival, and it models the principle that the *quality of attention* matters as much as the content discussed.

For the system: the facilitator may also name the evolutionary purpose — what is this group in service of, beyond the immediate agenda? This can be as simple as a sentence.

### Phase 2: Contextualize — question the question

Before solving a problem, spend real time ensuring the group is addressing the *right* problem.

- Is this a symptom or a root cause?
- What level of complexity are we dealing with — simple (just decide), complicated (analyze trade-offs), or complex (probe and sense)?
- What system dynamics are generating this issue?
- Is this a *problem to be solved* or a *polarity to be managed*? Tensions like speed vs. quality, centralization vs. decentralization, or candor vs. care cannot be resolved — only navigated. If the group is endlessly debating two valid poles, name the polarity and shift the question from "which is right?" to "how do we get the benefits of both while minimizing the downsides of both?"

This is where the difficult questions live. Frame it as a duty: "Our job right now is to find the questions we're avoiding."

**Output:** A shared map of the complexity landscape — tentative, explicit about uncertainty.

### Phase 3: Gather differentiated input

Collect perspectives without requiring them to agree with one another. Use rounds (a Sociocratic method): everyone speaks without interruption.

Critically, *explicitly invite the intelligence of each value system*:

- **To the Blue perspective:** "Does this align with our core principles and mandates? What is the proper procedure?"
- **To the Orange perspective:** "What does the data say? What are the strategic trade-offs and measurable outcomes?"
- **To the Green perspective:** "How will this impact people's sense of belonging? Who might feel left out or harmed?"
- **To the Red perspective:** "What are the real power dynamics? What needs force or decisiveness to move?"

Allow silence between contributions. Allow people to speak from their natural value system without correction. The facilitator's job is to hold the systemic frame, not to push anyone toward it.

**A note on power:** Rounds help, but structural power does not vanish because everyone gets a turn. When a CEO and a junior employee are in the same room, the junior's round will be shaped by that asymmetry. Concrete mitigations: anonymous written input before verbal rounds, pre-meeting contributions weighted equally regardless of role, or reverse hierarchy speaking order (junior voices first). Name the dynamic rather than pretending it isn't there.

### Phase 4: Asynchronous processing (when warranted)

For complex decisions, *break the meeting apart temporally*. Allow a set period — hours to days — for individuals to digest the mapping, let connections form below conscious awareness, and formulate integrative responses.

Use collaborative asynchronous tools (documents, shared boards) where people can add thoughts as they arise. This is countercultural in most organizations, which is exactly why it matters. Introverts, deep processors, and systems thinkers all need this.

Not every decision warrants this phase. The facilitator reads the complexity and decides.

### Phase 5: Integration sensing

Reconvene. The question is not yet "what should we do?" but:

> *What patterns do you notice across what's been said? What's emerging?*

This is the Turquoise move — listening for the signal beneath the positions. The facilitator acts as a weaver: "Our strategic analysis suggests speed. Our relational concern is about leaving people behind. Our principles require proper mandate. How might we design a solution that serves all three?"

**Optional Turquoise practice:** The "empty chair" — explicitly represent the voice of stakeholders not present: the wider ecosystem, future generations, the community downstream. Ask: "What would this decision look like from their perspective?"

**Other sensing practices** (use what fits the group's readiness):

- **Pattern recognition round:** "What's the one word or image that captures where you sense we are?" Notice what clusters.
- **The two-question check:** "What feels clear?" and "What feels unclear?" — the gap between them is often where the real decision lives.
- **Embodied check:** "How does this potential direction land in your body — not your analysis, your felt sense?" This requires sufficient safety but can surface information that cognition misses.
- **Pre-mortem:** "Imagine we made this decision and it failed. How did it fail?" This inverts the usual optimism bias and reveals risks the group is collectively avoiding.

### Phase 6: Decision mode selection

This is where Tier 2 most visibly differs from Tier 1. Instead of defaulting to a single decision method, the group *consciously selects* the mode that fits the decision:

- **Authority** — one person decides (appropriate when speed matters or expertise is concentrated)
- **Advice process** — one person decides after consulting affected parties and experts (not required to compromise). The decision-maker must be identified *before* advice is gathered — ambiguity here causes regression to Red power struggles or Green consensus-seeking.
- **Consent** — the proposal moves forward unless someone identifies a substantial objection (a systemic risk, not a preference). This replaces Green consensus without losing its inclusion value
- **Consensus** — full agreement, used sparingly for truly foundational decisions
- **Distributed experimentation** — multiple approaches are tried in parallel, with a review point

Name the mode explicitly, and name *why* it fits. This makes the process legible to every stage: Blue sees clear rules, Orange sees strategic logic, Green knows their voice counted, Red knows action will happen.

**Under pressure:** When decision latency creates systemic risk, collapse to authority or advice process immediately. The key distinction is between *true urgency* (the building is on fire) and *perceived urgency* (someone's discomfort with uncertainty). Tier 2 groups can pre-define fallback modes for crisis conditions, so the shift doesn't feel like a regression — it was anticipated.

### Phase 7: Translate back to Tier 1

A decision made in Tier 2 language may be illegible or alienating to people operating from Tier 1. The group explicitly discusses translation:

- "How do we communicate this to the parts of the organization that need to see clear procedure and authority?"
- "How do we frame it so the strategic value is visible?"
- "How do we convey the care and inclusion that went into the process?"

This is not manipulation — it is the recognition that the same decision can be truthfully described in multiple value languages, and that effective implementation requires meeting people where they are.

### Phase 8: Close with what remains

Don't pretend everything is wrapped up. Explicitly name:

- What was decided and in what mode
- What remains unresolved and when it will be revisited
- What feedback signals will tell the group if the decision is wrong — define 1–3 leading indicators (weak signals), not just lagging KPIs
- What thresholds or conditions would trigger reconsideration
- What worked well in the process itself

Every decision is a hypothesis in a living system. Frame it that way. Schedule a lightweight check-in (even 15 minutes) to ask: "What is the system telling us now that we couldn't see before?" This distinguishes Tier 2's "decide and learn" from Tier 1's "decide and defend."

## Including Tier 1 without collapsing

The trap for Tier 2 is designing a process so meta-aware that it becomes inaccessible, or so enamored with integration that it fails to actually decide.

The key principle: **each Tier 1 stage needs to see its own values reflected in the structure, even though the structure itself transcends any single stage.** These accommodations are not compromises — they are genuinely good features of a meeting that also happen to create entry points for each value system.

| Stage | Core need in meetings | How the protocol serves it |
|-------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| Beige | Safety, survival, physiological baseline | Acknowledgment of survival concerns without framing them as dysfunction; bio-breaks, movement, reasonable duration; not expecting "higher" functioning when basic safety is absent |
| Purple | Belonging, shared meaning | The grounding ritual of Phase 1; the closing |
| Red | Action, power, decisiveness | Explicit decision authority in Phase 6; space for direct truth-telling |
| Blue | Rules, sequence, clarity | Clear phase structure; named decision modes; documented outcomes |
| Orange | Efficiency, outcomes, logic | Contextualizing prevents wasted effort; strategic framing throughout |
| Green | Voice, care, inclusion | Rounds without interruption; silence for processing; relational questions |

A facilitator operating from Tier 2 practices *dynamic translation* — the same meeting, experienced through different layers. One person hears clear structure. Another hears strategic optimization. Another hears inclusive dialogue. The underlying system serves all of them.

Over time, participants may naturally begin to see the spiral — not because they are pushed, but because functional results build trust in the process.

## Common failure modes

Even well-intended Tier 2 attempts fail when:

- "Open discussion" collapses into Green chaos (endless processing, no decision)
- "Efficiency" collapses back into Orange dominance (the loudest analyst wins)
- "Structure" regresses into Blue rigidity (the protocol becomes sacred)
- Yellow becomes overly abstract (elegant models that no one can act on)
- Turquoise becomes vague and ungrounded (sensing without deciding)
- The group treats Tier 1 participants as "lesser" — which is itself a Tier 1 move in disguise

**The facilitator as single point of failure.** This protocol places enormous weight on the facilitator — reading complexity, selecting modes, translating dynamically. This creates specific shadow risks:

- **Over-steering** — covert control disguised as "holding the frame"
- **The map trap** — treating the spiral as *real* rather than as a model; diagnosing participants, labeling stages, subtly privileging those who "get it"
- **The integration trap** — forcing synthesis before its time, because the facilitator needs the elegance more than the system does
- **Spiritual bypassing** — Turquoise "sensing" used to avoid the hard, concrete work of deciding

The safeguard: the facilitator must be willing to be *seen through* by the group. If someone says "this feels like manipulation," the response is genuine curiosity, not a spiral diagnosis of their stage. The long-term goal is distributed facilitation — where anyone in the group can invoke a phase change, name a polarity, or call for an empty chair perspective. The facilitator works to make themselves unnecessary.

The antidote to all failure modes is the same: return to the question *"what actually serves the health of the whole system right now?"* — and be willing to hear an answer that challenges the group's self-image.

## A note on scale

This protocol as written fits groups of roughly 5–15 people. Beyond that, it needs structural adaptation:

- **Medium groups (15–40):** Break into subgroups with a shared frame, each with a facilitator. Phases 2 and 3 happen in parallel; integration sensing (Phase 5) becomes a "weaving team" that synthesizes across subgroups and reports back.
- **Large groups (40+):** The protocol becomes distributed. Phase 2 (contextualize) happens in writing beforehand. Phase 3 (input) uses representative sampling or nested Sociocratic circles. Decisions flow through delegation and modular decision units.
- **Asynchronous/distributed teams:** Phase 4 becomes the default mode rather than the exception. Shared digital spaces replace rooms. The facilitator role shifts toward curation and pattern-harvesting across contributions.

The fractal principle holds: the same pattern language works at every scale, but the *medium* changes.

## Adaptation for low-trust and adversarial contexts

The protocol as described assumes a minimum of shared purpose and psychological safety. Many real coordination problems — between organizations, factions, jurisdictions, or stakeholders with conflicting interests — don't have that foundation. The protocol still applies, but several phases need to shift.

**From shared decisions to coordinated independent decisions.** In adversarial contexts, the actors often don't share a decision space at all. Each party will decide independently. The best achievable outcome is not agreement but *mutual predictability* — a stable configuration of independent actions where no party has incentive to escalate. Phase 6 (decision mode selection) becomes less about choosing a shared mode and more about mapping: "What set of independent actions leads to system stability?" This is coordination without cooperation — and it's often enough.

**Catastrophe boundaries replace grounding.** The Phase 1 question ("where is your attention?") requires vulnerability that adversarial contexts won't support. Replace it with: *"What outcome must you avoid at all costs?"* This reveals constraints without requiring trust, and it often surfaces a shared catastrophe boundary — a zone that no party wants to enter — which becomes the basis for coordination. Parties that cannot agree on what they want can often agree on what they must prevent.

**The elastic zone.** After a disruption — a broken agreement, an unexpected escalation, a betrayal of trust — there is usually a narrow band where response is still possible without triggering full collapse. Tier 2 facilitation in these moments asks not "how do we respond?" but *"what level of response keeps us inside the zone where recovery remains possible?"* This applies in organizations as much as in geopolitics: the meeting after a leadership betrayal, the negotiation after a partner defects from a deal.

**Every action carries a signal layer.** In multi-actor systems, decisions have two dimensions: their material impact and their *interpretive* impact — how other actors will read them. Phase 7 (translate back to Tier 1) already touches this, but in adversarial contexts it becomes the primary concern throughout the entire process. The question is not just "what do we do?" but "what does what we do *mean* to the other actors in the system?" A response framed as "proportional and complete" produces a fundamentally different system dynamic than the same material action framed as "the first step."

These adaptations don't replace the core protocol — they extend it into terrain where trust is scarce and the coordination problem is between actors who may never share a room, let alone a worldview.

## Starting small

A mixed-stage group does not need the full protocol on day one. Even a single element can shift the field:

- Introduce **structured silence** (2–3 minutes of individual reflection before discussion)
- Ask **one systemic question** at the opening ("What are we not seeing?")
- Make **decision mode explicit** ("We're using consent for this, not consensus — here's why")
- Try the **advice process** for one real decision

These are low-cost interventions that produce immediate, tangible results — and they model the possibility of a different way of coordinating without requiring anyone to adopt Tier 2 language.

**The organizational immune response.** Be aware that introducing even small Tier 2 elements into a deeply Tier 1 culture may trigger resistance. An Orange group may interpret silence as inefficiency. A Blue group may see flexible decision modes as a lack of proper authority. This is normal and predictable.

The most effective approach in resistant contexts is *stealth implementation* — translate the practice into the group's native value language rather than announcing a new methodology. You don't need to say "we are gathering differentiated input." You can say "to get the best data quickly, let's go around the table once without cross-talk." Speak Orange to Orange. Speak Blue to Blue. The underlying Tier 2 structure does its work whether or not anyone names it.

**What to expect in the first three meetings:** The first time, structured silence will feel awkward and the systemic question may produce blank looks. The second time, one or two people will use the silence productively and the question will generate a surprising insight. By the third time, the group will begin to protect these elements — they'll notice when they're missing. That's the signal that the soil has shifted.
